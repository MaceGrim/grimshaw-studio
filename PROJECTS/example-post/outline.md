# The Honest Cost of AI Prototypes -- Outline

## Hook

Open with the moment every AI consultant knows: the client saw a demo, the CEO is excited, and now someone has to explain that the shiny thing they just watched won't work with their actual data without months of work. Ground it in a specific (anonymized) engagement. No hype, no apology -- just the reality of the gap.

## Sections

### 1. The Demo Is Not the Prototype
- The 2-hour demo vs. the 6-week prototype
- Why vendor demos look flawless (curated data, controlled environment, cherry-picked outputs)
- The moment you plug in real data and accuracy drops 30 points
- Frame: this is normal, not a failure

### 2. Where the Time Actually Goes (with visualization)
- Break down a typical 10-week prototype engagement
- Data work: cleaning, mapping, access negotiations (weeks 1-3)
- Model work: the part everyone thinks is the whole thing (weeks 3-5)
- Integration: connecting to real systems, real workflows (weeks 5-7)
- Iteration: what happens when users actually touch it (weeks 7-10)
- Embed the cost breakdown visualization here

### 3. The Iteration Tax
- First version survival rate: roughly 0%
- Three types of pivots: data pivots, UX pivots, scope pivots
- Why this is actually good news (you learn what matters)
- Real example: document processing tool that became a triage system

### 4. Managing Expectations Without Killing Momentum
- The board meeting problem: CEO saw a demo, wants production by Q3
- Framework: "Here's what we'll know by when"
- Milestone-based communication instead of feature-based promises
- The trusted performer test as an evaluation framework

### 5. What Good Looks Like
- Practical framework for a healthy prototype process
- Stage 1: Prove the concept works on your data (not benchmark data)
- Stage 2: Put it alongside your best performers
- Stage 3: Let users break it (they will)
- Stage 4: Decide if the value justifies production investment

## Takeaway

Building AI prototypes is expensive, slow, and uncertain. That's not a bug -- that's how you build things that actually work. The organizations that succeed are the ones that budget for reality, not for the demo. End with a concrete action: before your next AI initiative, map the full cost -- not just the model, but the data work, the integration, the iteration, and the change management. If the number still makes sense, you've got a real project.
