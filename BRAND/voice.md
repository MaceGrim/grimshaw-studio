# Voice Guidelines

## Tone:

Varies by context, but these are the defaults:

- Casual ────◆───── Rigorous *(middle - thorough when it matters, conversational when it doesn't)*
- Playful ─────────◆ Serious *(enterprise = serious, experiments = playful)*
- Accessible ◆──────── Technical *(accessible first - anyone can follow, depth available for those who want it)*

## Signature Moves

### 1. Systems thinking
Connect AI work to the broader system it lives inside - organizational incentives, community dynamics, data pipelines, policy constraints. Never treat a tool as isolated from the people and processes around it.

**Example:** "Before you pick an LLM, map out who actually touches the output. The model is maybe 20% of the problem. The other 80% is change management, data quality, and whether your team trusts the thing enough to use it."

### 2. Visual/Artifact-First
Lead with the demo. Show the thing working before explaining how it works. Screenshots, videos, live examples > walls of text.

**Example:** "Here's the dashboard we built for the tribal health department in two weeks. Notice how the filters work - that's not a mockup, that's live data. Now let me explain how we got there."

### 3. Grounded skepticism
Start by naming the real problem or fatigue before diving into solutions. Question assumptions, call out gaps between marketing and reality, but always follow through with what actually works. Not cynicism - honest assessment with constructive follow-through.

**Example:** "Every vendor demo looks flawless. Then you plug in your own data and the accuracy drops 30 points. That's not a bug - it's the gap between curated benchmarks and messy reality. Here's how we closed that gap on our last engagement."

### 4. Storytelling from Experience
Real examples from consulting engagements, tribal community, daily work. Not hypotheticals - things that actually happened. Ground abstract concepts in specific moments.

**Example:** "Last month a tribal council member asked me, 'Can AI help us predict which families need support before crisis hits?' That's not a tech question - that's a sovereignty question about who controls the data and who benefits from the predictions."

### 5. Practical Playbooks
End with actionable advice. Not "consider your options" but "put your AI outputs alongside your most trusted performers."

**Example:** "Step one: sit your best analyst next to the AI output for two weeks. Step two: have them red-team every recommendation. Step three: only then do you start trusting it to run without a human checkpoint."

## Taboos:

- **AI hype/breathlessness** - no "revolutionary," "game-changing," AGI timelines
- **Savior framing** - no savior narratives; Indigenous communities lead their own work; I'm not rescuing anyone
- **Performative futurism** - no abstract futures without concrete present
- **Generic advice** - "leverage AI for competitive advantage" says nothing
- **Vendor pitches** - I'm not selling tools, I'm sharing what works

## Nuance:

Taboos can be broken when:
- Quoting others to critique them
- Genuine excitement about a specific result (earned, not performed)
- Discussing what communities actually asked for (their framing, not mine)

## Example Sentences

1. "It's annoying to have to talk about AI. It's everywhere even when we don't want it to be, but it's also important to intentionally discuss what AI could mean for our communities and our future."

2. "AI projects live and die in the trenches. It can be a combination of fatigue from the barrage of tools, or a lack of clarity on 'what does this thing actually do for us?'"

3. "If you're starting with AI, you want your AI outputs right alongside your most trusted performers. They'll be able to tell you exactly where the tool isn't working."

4. "With responsible deployment, AI can be a huge boon for resource-strapped communities, and Indigenous people are well positioned to lead us all toward a future in which AI works with humanity."
